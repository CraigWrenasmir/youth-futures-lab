1What it is

A one-day civic design challenge for high school students, run in the TEDx spirit. Students work in small "consultancy teams" advising a fictional council. Each team picks one part of city life that affects young people, comes up with an idea to improve it, makes something visual to explain it, then delivers a 2-minute TED-style pitch to a friendly listening panel.

The phrase we keep coming back to is "design a smarter and kinder Newcastle." The whole day hangs off those two words.
SmartSystems, technology, transport, infrastructure, planning — a city that works well. This is what makes it feel like a real consultancy brief, not a craft activity.
KindCare, belonging, safety, accessibility, affordability, mental health — a city that cares about people. This is what stops it becoming a cold techno-utopia of LED benches and facial-recognition bins.

And why it's a TEDx thing: we're not running a competition so much as giving young people a stage for ideas worth sharing, about a city that usually plans for them rather than with them. When a 15-year-old says "there are places for adults to spend money, but not many places for us to simply exist", that's a real civic insight. The day exists to catch those before they evaporate.

3The moving parts

None of this is accidental — each of these is here so that no team can get properly lost, and so nothing on the day depends on us improvising.

10 focus areas

Teams choose from a teen-centred list (transport, hangout spaces, cost, safety, quiet spaces, creativity and so on). Choosing gives them ownership, and having a list means nobody starts from a blank page.

3 required outputs

An idea, something visual, and a 2-minute pitch. Having concrete things to make keeps every hour pointed somewhere.

The 3-line pitch spine

"Right now… / But what if… / Imagine if…" Simple enough that every team can remember it under pressure. Taught at 10:50, rehearsed at 1:00.

8 idea checks

Who's it for? Where? How is it smart? How is it kind? What would the council need to do? Teams self-test against these all through Workshop 2.

The remix moment

~11:45: "Steal one idea kindly" — envoys visit other tables and bring one idea home. Breaks the room open, lifts weaker concepts, makes it collaborative rather than competitive.

Lunch mini-expo

Work stays on the tables so students, teachers and guests can wander around and look at everything. Costs us nothing and gives the room a lift going into the afternoon.

Listening panel, not judges

Panel guests get a printed card: listen for what's practical, what adults might miss, what's worth exploring. One warm comment per pitch, no scoring.

8 recognitions, no winner

Most Imaginative Future, Kindest City Idea, Strongest Youth Voice and so on. Different kinds of thinking get seen, and nobody goes home having "lost".

Timers on every slide

The facilitator deck has countdown timers built into each activity slide, plus a 2:00 pitch timer we reset between teams, so the schedule largely looks after itself.

The rescue question

If a team has drifted, one question usually brings them back: "What changes because your idea exists?" It's on the crew run card.

4Roles on the day

The day runs comfortably with 4–6 people. One person can hold two of the lighter roles.

MC / HostDelivers the scripted opening (it's on the slides), hosts the pitches, names the themes at reflection. Craig and/or Maggie.
Deck driver / TimekeeperRuns the facilitator deck, starts the built-in timers, resets the 2:00 pitch timer between teams. The deck does the hard work — this is arrow keys and one button.
Roaming coaches ×2Circulate during workshops with the run-card questions. Never give answers — open doors. Watch for stuck teams, five-idea teams, and one-voice teams.
Registration & logisticsMorning desk, team allocation to tables, table kits topped up, lunch flow. Quiet after 1:00.
CapturePhotographs every poster and pitch, and jots down quotable student lines for the reflection and for TEDxNewy follow-up. The posters and photos are what we'll have to show for the day afterwards, so this role matters more than it sounds.
Panel wranglerGreets panel guests, hands them the listening card, briefs them over lunch, keeps their comments to one per pitch. Can double with registration.

7If things wobble

Fewer students than expectedThe format scales down cleanly — 6 teams works as well as 14. Merge part-teams at registration; nothing else changes.
A team is stuckCoaches carry the rescue question: "What changes because your idea exists?" Plus: "Pick the problem that actually annoys you."
Running behindThe buffer is in the pitch block (1:30–2:20 holds 14 pitches with room to spare). Trim rehearsal to 20 min before touching anything else.
A team won't pitchThey can present as a "poster walk" — the MC interviews them at their table for 2 minutes. Same three lines, gentler stage.
Tech failsThe whole day survives without the deck: focus cards, worksheets and pitch templates are on paper at every table. A phone timer replaces the screen timer.
Panel guest goes judge-yPanel wrangler resets gently at the break: "one warm observation per pitch." The listening card frames this in writing from the start.
The magic isn't "which team has the best idea?" — it's students discovering their observations are politically, socially, and creatively meaningful.
