TEDxNewy · Youth Futures Lab · Friday 7 August 2026

Smart + Kind
Newcastle

A team-based civic design challenge for high school students, ending in TED-style pitches. Students act as youth consultants advising the council.

Fri 7 Aug 2026 9:30 am – 2:30 pm ~7 schools · 60–75 students 10–14 teams of 5–6 2-minute pitches
What we'll tell the students Today, you are consultants working for the council. Your job is to choose one part of city life and design a new idea, improvement, service, space, campaign, or invention that would make Newcastle both smarter and kinder for young people.
1

The Big Idea

The key phrase is: design a smarter and kinder Newcastle.

Smart

Brings in systems, technology, transport, infrastructure, data, planning and innovation. A smart city works well.

Kind

Stops it becoming a cold techno-utopia with LED benches and facial-recognition bins. Brings in care, belonging, safety, accessibility, creativity, cost, mental health, and the lived experience of young people. A kind city cares about people.

The future is not only something adults build for young people. It is something young people should help imagine, shape, challenge, and explain.

The day should feel less like a competition and more like a civic imagination lab. The magic is not just "which team has the best idea?" — it's letting students discover that their observations are politically, socially, and creatively meaningful. A 15-year-old saying, "There are places for adults to spend money, but not many places for us to simply exist," is already a civic insight. The event should catch those insights before they evaporate.

Teams & the Task

With around seven schools and roughly ten students per school: 60–75 students → 10–14 teams of 5–6. Each table becomes a consultancy team. Schools sending ten students split into two teams.

Grouping instinct

Start students in school-based teams unless Maggie has a strong reason to mix them immediately. For a fast day, comfort matters. Then build in a cross-pollination moment (the 11:45 remix) so they still encounter other ideas.

Every team must produce three things

  • 1. A future city idea — a new space, service, system, invention, campaign, policy, event, app, map, or improvement.
  • 2. A tangible asset — something visual or concrete that helps people understand the idea.
  • 3. A 2-minute TED-style pitch — a clear, persuasive explanation of the problem, the idea, and the future it creates.

Workshop 1: See the City Differently

10:00–11:00 am. Goal: students understand the challenge, choose a focus area, identify a real problem, and begin shaping an idea.

10:00 – 10:10

Welcome & framing

Craig / Maggie introduce: the TEDx spirit (ideas worth sharing), Youth Futures Lab (young people as future thinkers), smart + kind Newcastle, and the consultant role-play.

"A smart city is not just a city with better technology. A smart city should also be kinder, fairer, safer, more creative, and more human."

10:10 – 10:20

Choose a focus area

Each team chooses one of the ten focus areas — focus cards printed on each table.

Prompt: "Pick a part of city life that actually affects you or people your age."

10:20 – 10:35

Problem discovery

Teams answer:

  • What is the current problem?
  • Who does it affect?
  • Where does it happen?
  • What makes it frustrating, unfair, unsafe, boring, expensive, inaccessible, or overlooked?
  • What do adults misunderstand about this issue? ← keep this one; it gives students permission to speak from lived experience
10:35 – 10:50

Idea generation

Teams generate several possible solutions, then choose one. They can design: a place · a service · an app or digital tool · a campaign · an event · a new rule or policy · a map · a transport improvement · a public artwork · a physical object · a new kind of youth space.

10:50 – 11:00

Mini TED pitch lesson

Teach the three-line pitch spine: Right now… (the problem) → But what if… (your idea) → Imagine if… (what changes). Simple, memorable, with a lovely rhetorical snap.

6

Workshop 2: Build the Future Proposal

11:15 am–12:15 pm. Goal: teams develop their idea into something visible, persuasive, and pitch-ready.

Team outputs — at least one of

Concept poster · map · sketch or labelled diagram · service design plan · app screen mock-up · advertisement · campaign slogan · prototype model · before/after city plan · storyboard · pitch slide · one-page council proposal.

The eight idea checks

Every team should be able to answer — this is the practical spine of the whole day:

  • 1. What is the problem?
  • 2. What is your solution?
  • 3. Who is it for?
  • 4. Where would it happen?
  • 5. How is it smart?
  • 6. How is it kind?
  • 7. What would the council need to do?
  • 8. What would success look like?
~11:45 am · The Remix Moment

"Send one or two people from your team to visit another table. Find one idea you like, bring it back, and use it to improve your own proposal."

Or simply: "Steal one idea kindly." Five minutes is enough. It breaks the room open, improves weaker concepts, and makes the event feel collaborative rather than purely competitive.

Lunch · Mini-Expo

Invite teams to leave their visual materials on tables so students, teachers and guests can wander through the ideas casually. The room becomes a small exhibition.

7

The Pitch

1:00–1:30 pm practice & coaching · 2 minutes per team.

Right now…Describe the current problem.
But what if…Introduce your idea.
Imagine if…Explain the future your idea creates.
Smarter because…Explain the practical / system benefit.
Kinder because…Explain the human / community benefit.

Coaching points

Start with the problem, not the product
Use one clear idea
Speak to humans, not robots in blazers
Use a real example: "Imagine you're 15 and…"
Keep the language simple
One person doesn't have to do all the talking
Use the poster/map/prototype as support, not as a script
Finish with a strong sentence
Our idea matters because young people deserve a city that doesn't just include them in the future, but listens to them now.
8

Final Showcase

1:30–2:20 pm. With 10–14 teams at 2 minutes each: 20–28 minutes of pitch time. Add transitions and applause and it's tight but possible.

  • 2-minute pitch
  • 30 seconds transition / applause
  • Optional: one panel comment — not a full Q&A
A listening panel, not a judging panel

Ask council guests to listen for: ideas that are practical · ideas that reveal something adults might miss · ideas that could be explored further. Avoid anything that feels like harsh judging.

Recognition categories — many ways to shine

Rather than a single "winner", several recognitions keep the TEDx spirit warm and generous — different types of intelligence get seen:

Most Imaginative Future Smartest System Idea Kindest City Idea Strongest Youth Voice Best Pitch Best Visual Concept Most Practical Council Proposal Best "We Hadn't Thought of That" Idea

Facilitation Notes — Craig & Maggie

Your role across the day is not to give students answers. It's to ask the little hinge questions that open doors.

Roaming questions

Who is this idea for?
Where would this happen?
What problem are you really solving?
How would a teenager experience this?
What would make this kinder?
What would make this smarter?
What would the council need to believe for this to happen?
What is the one-sentence version of your idea?
What would someone see, hear, or feel if this existed?
What is the part adults might not understand?
The killer question — rescues almost any wandering group

"What changes because your idea exists?"

Opening the day — suggested script

Welcome to Youth Futures Lab.

Today, we are asking you to act as consultants to the council. Your job is to design an idea for a smarter and kinder Newcastle.

Smart matters. Cities need good systems, good transport, good information, good design, and good decisions. But smart is not enough.

A city also needs to be kind. It needs to feel safe, welcoming, creative, inclusive, affordable, accessible, and human.

So today, your challenge is to choose one part of city life that affects young people, notice what could be better, design a new idea, and pitch it back to us.

You might design a space, a service, an app, a campaign, a map, an event, a public artwork, a new rule, or something none of us have thought of yet. The important thing is that your idea should help people see Newcastle differently.

By the end of the day, your team will give a short pitch using three lines: Right now… But what if… Imagine if…

That is all a good idea needs to begin: a real problem, a shift in thinking, and a picture of a better future.

Teacher Guidance Letter

Dear teachers,

We are looking forward to welcoming your students to the TEDx Youth Futures Lab on Friday 7 August 2026.

The day will invite high school students to work in small consultancy teams to design ideas for a smarter and kinder Newcastle. Students will choose a focus area connected to young people's lives, develop a future-focused proposal, create a simple visual or practical asset, and deliver a short TED-style pitch at the end of the day.

Students do not need to prepare a finished idea before they arrive. However, it would be helpful if they spend a little time beforehand thinking about Newcastle from a young person's point of view.

Before the event, students may like to consider:

  • What parts of Newcastle work well for young people?
  • What parts of the city feel difficult, expensive, unsafe, boring, inaccessible, or unwelcoming?
  • Where do young people currently spend time?
  • Where do young people feel they belong?
  • What do adults often misunderstand about young people's experience of the city?
  • What would make Newcastle easier, safer, more creative, more inclusive, or more enjoyable for teenagers?
  • What would make them want to stay connected to Newcastle in the future?

On the day, students will be able to choose from focus areas such as transport, places to hang out, digital life, safety, cost, learning beyond school, mental health and quiet spaces, food and social spaces, creative expression, and the future of Newcastle for young people.

Students are encouraged to bring curiosity, imagination, and a willingness to work with others. They may also bring a device if appropriate, although all activities will be designed so that students can participate with basic materials provided on the day.

We are especially interested in ideas that are both smart and kind: ideas that improve how the city works, while also making it more inclusive, human, creative, and welcoming.

Kind regards,

Craig and Maggie

Before the day: start noticing your city

At Youth Futures Lab, you will work in a small team as a group of young consultants advising the council.

Your challenge: design an idea that would make Newcastle smarter and kinder for young people.

A smart city works well. A kind city cares about people.

Before the event, start noticing the city around you. Think about:

  • Where do young people feel welcome?
  • Where do young people feel unwelcome?
  • How easy is it to get around?
  • What costs too much?
  • Where do you go when you want to be with friends?
  • Where do you go when you need quiet?
  • What places feel safe?
  • What places feel uncomfortable?
  • What would make Newcastle better for teenagers?
  • What do adults often miss or misunderstand?

You do not need to arrive with a finished idea. Just bring observations.

On the day, your team will choose one focus area, design a future idea, create something visual or practical to explain it, and pitch your idea to the room. Your pitch will answer:

Right now… What is the problem?
But what if… What is your idea?
Imagine if… What would change?

The best ideas do not have to be huge. Sometimes a small change can help people see the whole city differently.

Run Card · Fri 7 Aug 2026
Times · cues · the questions that rescue tables

Timeline & cues

  • 9:30 — Registration. Holding slide up. Teams of 5–6 settle at tables.
  • 10:00 — Welcome. Opening script (slide 2–4): TEDx spirit → consultant brief → smart + kind.
  • 10:15 — WS1. Choose focus area (8 min timer) → problem discovery (15) → idea generation (15) → pitch lesson.
  • 11:00 — Break (15 min timer).
  • 11:15 — WS2. Build (60 min timer). Keep the 8 idea checks slide handy.
  • ~11:45 — REMIX CUE: "Steal one idea kindly" (5 min timer). Don't skip this.
  • 12:15 — Lunch. Announce mini-expo: materials stay on tables.
  • 1:00 — Rehearsal + coaching (30 min). Roam with the questions below.
  • 1:30 — Pitches. 2 min each + 30 sec transition. Reset pitch timer between teams. Optional single panel comment.
  • 2:20 — Reflection: name the themes. Recognitions. 2:30 hard finish.

Roaming questions (don't give answers — open doors)

  • Who is this idea for? Where would it happen?
  • What problem are you really solving?
  • How would a teenager experience this?
  • What would make this kinder? Smarter?
  • What's the one-sentence version of your idea?
  • What's the part adults might not understand?
  • The rescue question: "What changes because your idea exists?"

Watch for

  • Teams stuck choosing → "Pick the one that actually annoys you."
  • Teams with 5 ideas at 11:30 → "Choose one. Steal from the rest."
  • One-voice teams → "Who's presenting which line?"
  • Insights worth catching → photograph posters, note quotes for reflection.
